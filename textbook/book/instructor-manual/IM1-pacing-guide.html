IM-1 — Pacing Guide

Companion documents. This guide governs the clock. For how each deliverable is scored, see IM-2 (Grading Rubrics); for the errors to watch as the clock runs, see IM-3 (Common Student Pitfalls); for the layered-mentorship rhythm and how all-hands sessions are run, see IM-4 (Mentor-Session Facilitation & Guest Lectures); for anchor work and answer keys, IM-5; for the access, compute, licensing, and waiver constraints that shape any schedule you build, IM-6 (Equity, Access & Compliance). All cross-references are by name so this manual survives reordering.

The ASSIP 2026 cohort runs full-time Monday–Friday, 9:00 am–5:00 pm, from Monday June 22, 2026 through Wednesday August 12, 2026 (the Symposium). Orientation is Thursday June 18, 2026. Two federal holidays fall in the window — Friday June 19, 2026 (Juneteenth observance), the day before orientation, and Friday July 3, 2026 (Independence Day observance) — and the cohort takes those days off. Prof. Gao’s 2026 ASSIP slot is REMOTE only: every block in this guide is run over Zoom, recorded, and re-watchable from the cohort wiki.

This gives eight active program weeks (Jun 22 – Aug 12) with 37 working weekdays minus 1 holiday = 36 active weekdays, plus the half-day Symposium on Wed Aug 12. The textbook was built for exactly an eight-week intensive at roughly this hour count, so under the ASSIP calendar the eight book weeks map one-to-one onto the eight program weeks — no compression, no merging. What this guide gives you is the daily shape of each week: which block runs the curriculum, which block runs the mentor research project the camp deliverables actually demand, and which block runs the cohort sync that keeps a remote 14-student cohort glued together.

1. The eight-week clock

The eight weeks are not eight parallel modules; they are a single load-bearing arc, and the order is not negotiable. Weeks 1–4 build the toolkit from the ground up: probability and the logic of inference (W1), the OLS engine and its standard-error pathologies (W2), causal inference from potential outcomes through matching and instruments (W3), and the modern quasi-experimental toolkit — DiD, RD, synthetic control, shift-share — together with the recent literature showing the obvious estimators are often wrong (W4). Weeks 5–6 turn from running estimators to reading them: one empirical paper per day under a fixed Reader’s-Guide anatomy (W5), then text-as-data and a full module on using LLMs as a research co-pilot without fooling yourself (W6). Weeks 7–8 are the capstone: question to pre-analysis plan (W7), then execution, robustness, writing, the poster, the abstract, and the eight-minute talk at the Symposium (W8).

Each week earns the next. You cannot read a weak-instrument disaster in Week 5 if you did not derive 2SLS in Week 3; you cannot stress-test your own DiD in Week 8 if you did not meet the staggered-adoption crisis in Week 4. The dependency order is fixed.

A useful way to hold the whole arc in mind: Weeks 1–4 answer “what is a defensible number?”, Weeks 5–6 answer “how do I judge someone else’s number, including a machine’s?”, and Weeks 7–8 answer “can I produce a defensible number of my own and survive a referee, a Symposium audience, and the JSSR editor?” The terminal assessment (W8) is the only thing the camp was ever building toward; every earlier week is rehearsal for it.

2. The ASSIP daily block structure

The ASSIP day is eight hours, 9:00 am – 5:00 pm, with a one-hour lunch (12:00–1:00). Within those eight hours the cohort runs three repeating blocks, every weekday, plus a fourth block that lives only on Monday mornings:

  • 9:00–12:00 — Curriculum block. Three hours of book content: chapter read-along, notebook live work, and problem-set clinic. This is where the textbook’s daily rhythm lives — one chapter + notebook + problem set per day, five days a week, five chapters per book-week.
  • 12:00–1:00 — Lunch. Hard break. Off-camera. Resists the temptation to schedule “working lunches” — a remote cohort burns out faster than a residential one, and the lunch break is the single biggest dropout-prevention lever you control.
  • 1:00–4:00 — Mentor research block. Three hours of work on the student’s own capstone project — data acquisition, EDA, replication, identification design, PAP drafting, robustness, writing, poster layout. Pod sub-leaders (the four PhD/RA mentors) hold open Zoom rooms during this block; Prof. Gao drops in by rotation. The mentor research block is the engine that produces the Symposium poster, the JSSR abstract, and the optional full paper.
  • 4:00–5:00 — Cohort sync. A one-hour shared close to the day: ten-minute pod standups led by each sub-leader (each pod reports what they built, what is blocked, what they will do tomorrow); forty-minute rotating element — Tue/Thu peer-review pairs walking each other through their notebooks, Wed a guest-lecture or skill clinic, Fri a fifteen-minute show-and-tell where one randomly drawn student demos the week’s deliverable to the cohort. Mondays the cohort sync is replaced by the all-hands mentor session that opens the morning (see below).
  • Monday 9:00–10:00 — All-hands mentor session with Prof. Gao (IM-4). This sits inside the Monday curriculum block, pushing the chapter/notebook/PS work to 10:00–12:00 on Mondays only. Eight Mondays, eight all-hands sessions, each anchored to one of Prof. Gao’s papers (see IM-4 for the topic sequence). This is the cohort’s intellectual anchor of the week.

The week therefore costs each student 40 contact hours (8 hours × 5 days), of which roughly 15 are curriculum, 15 are mentor research, and 5 are cohort sync; the remaining 5 land in Monday’s all-hands mentor session and the buffer that Friday’s show-and-tell creates. This is the magnitude the eight-week book was written to.

Why the day is split this way

Three pedagogical pressures shape the schedule. First, morning curriculum, afternoon research matches the cognitive science: declarative learning (reading a chapter, deriving an estimator) goes best when working memory is freshest, and applied work (debugging your own pipeline, refining a threats table) is more resilient to mid-day fatigue. Second, the mentor research block is the camp’s actual deliverable engine — the Symposium poster, the JSSR abstract, and the 8-minute talk are all built in this block, not in the curriculum block; giving it three protected hours a day prevents the typical research-program failure where students “ran out of time” because the curriculum ate the project. Third, the cohort sync is the remote-format anti-isolation mechanism — fourteen students working alone in fourteen different homes is the worst-case dropout scenario; the 4:00 pm end-of-day check-in is what makes Tuesday morning’s start-up reliable.

4. The layered-mentorship model

ASSIP 2026’s cohort is 14 students — at the high end of typical ASSIP group sizes (2–15). One faculty mentor cannot give 14 remote students the same depth of weekly attention a 4-student in-person group would receive; the cohort is therefore organized as a layered mentorship:

  • Prof. Lei Gao is the faculty mentor of record. He runs the Monday all-hands mentor session (9:00–10:00, recorded), is in the room for the Tuesday dry-run defense in Week 8, sets the Symposium poster standards, and rotates through pod afternoon office hours across the week.
  • Four PhD / RA sub-leaders (one per pod) are the daily owners of their pod’s progress. Each sub-leader: runs the 4:00 pm pod standup, holds the open drop-in office hour during the 1:00–4:00 mentor research block, grades their pod’s daily problem sets against IM-2, escalates any pod concern to Prof. Gao the same day, and is the named co-mentor on each of their pod’s three or four capstone posters.
  • Four pods of three or four students each are the working unit. Students stay in their pod Monday–Friday for standups and problem-set clinics; pods are reshuffled exactly once, at the start of W4, to break early cliques and to balance project topics — the second-half pod composition is the one that runs through the Symposium.

The layered model is what makes a 14-student remote cohort work. It also makes IM-4 — the mentor-session facilitation chapter — load-bearing in a new way: Prof. Gao runs the Monday all-hands session and is the escalation backstop, but the sub-leaders are the ones running daily research mentorship, and IM-4 now documents both layers.

## 1. The eight-week clock

The eight weeks are not eight parallel modules; they are a single load-bearing arc, and the order is
not negotiable. Weeks 1–4 build the toolkit from the ground up: probability and the logic of
inference (W1), the OLS engine and its standard-error pathologies (W2), causal inference from
potential outcomes through matching and instruments (W3), and the modern quasi-experimental toolkit —
DiD, RD, synthetic control, shift-share — together with the recent literature showing the obvious
estimators are often wrong (W4). Weeks 5–6 turn from *running* estimators to *reading* them: one
empirical paper per day under a fixed Reader's-Guide anatomy (W5), then text-as-data and a full
module on using LLMs as a research co-pilot without fooling yourself (W6). Weeks 7–8 are the
capstone: question to pre-analysis plan (W7), then execution, robustness, writing, the poster, the
abstract, and the eight-minute talk at the Symposium (W8).

Each week earns the next. You cannot read a weak-instrument disaster in Week 5 if you did not derive
2SLS in Week 3; you cannot stress-test your own DiD in Week 8 if you did not meet the
staggered-adoption crisis in Week 4. The dependency order is fixed.

A useful way to hold the whole arc in mind: Weeks 1–4 answer "what is a defensible number?", Weeks
5–6 answer "how do I judge someone else's number, including a machine's?", and Weeks 7–8 answer "can
I produce a defensible number of my own and survive a referee, a Symposium audience, and the JSSR
editor?" The terminal assessment (W8) is the only thing the camp was ever building toward; every
earlier week is rehearsal for it.

## 2. The ASSIP daily block structure

The ASSIP day is **eight hours, 9:00 am – 5:00 pm**, with a one-hour lunch (12:00–1:00). Within those
eight hours the cohort runs three repeating blocks, every weekday, plus a fourth block that lives
only on Monday mornings:

- **9:00–12:00 — Curriculum block.** Three hours of book content: chapter read-along, notebook live
  work, and problem-set clinic. This is where the textbook's daily rhythm lives — one chapter +
  notebook + problem set per day, five days a week, five chapters per book-week.
- **12:00–1:00 — Lunch.** Hard break. Off-camera. Resists the temptation to schedule "working
  lunches" — a remote cohort burns out faster than a residential one, and the lunch break is the
  single biggest dropout-prevention lever you control.
- **1:00–4:00 — Mentor research block.** Three hours of work on the student's own capstone project —
  data acquisition, EDA, replication, identification design, PAP drafting, robustness, writing,
  poster layout. Pod sub-leaders (the four PhD/RA mentors) hold open Zoom rooms during this block;
  Prof. Gao drops in by rotation. The mentor research block is the engine that produces the
  Symposium poster, the JSSR abstract, and the optional full paper.
- **4:00–5:00 — Cohort sync.** A one-hour shared close to the day: ten-minute pod standups led by
  each sub-leader (each pod reports what they built, what is blocked, what they will do tomorrow);
  forty-minute rotating element — Tue/Thu peer-review pairs walking each other through their
  notebooks, Wed a guest-lecture or skill clinic, Fri a fifteen-minute show-and-tell where one
  randomly drawn student demos the week's deliverable to the cohort. Mondays the cohort sync is
  replaced by the **all-hands mentor session** that opens the morning (see below).
- **Monday 9:00–10:00 — All-hands mentor session with Prof. Gao** (IM-4). This sits *inside* the
  Monday curriculum block, pushing the chapter/notebook/PS work to 10:00–12:00 on Mondays only.
  Eight Mondays, eight all-hands sessions, each anchored to one of Prof. Gao's papers (see IM-4 for
  the topic sequence). This is the cohort's intellectual anchor of the week.

The week therefore costs each student **40 contact hours** (8 hours × 5 days), of which roughly 15
are curriculum, 15 are mentor research, and 5 are cohort sync; the remaining 5 land in Monday's
all-hands mentor session and the buffer that Friday's show-and-tell creates. This is the magnitude
the eight-week book was written to.

### Why the day is split this way

Three pedagogical pressures shape the schedule. First, **morning curriculum, afternoon research**
matches the cognitive science: declarative learning (reading a chapter, deriving an estimator) goes
best when working memory is freshest, and applied work (debugging your own pipeline, refining a
threats table) is more resilient to mid-day fatigue. Second, **the mentor research block is the
camp's actual deliverable engine** — the Symposium poster, the JSSR abstract, and the 8-minute talk
are all built in this block, not in the curriculum block; giving it three protected hours a day
prevents the typical research-program failure where students "ran out of time" because the curriculum
ate the project. Third, **the cohort sync is the remote-format anti-isolation mechanism** — fourteen
students working alone in fourteen different homes is the worst-case dropout scenario; the 4:00 pm
end-of-day check-in is what makes Tuesday morning's start-up reliable.

  remediation as async work for the orientation weekend to anyone who misses the
  calculus/probability/Python anchors.
- Pin the environment (`python=3.11` + the CONVENTIONS §5 stack) and confirm every notebook runs on
  a fresh env on the Zoom shared display.
- Stand up **GitHub Classroom** and the capstone repo template (Lab 7 / Appendix B.2) so the
  `pap-filed` tag workflow is ready by W7.

Each day:

- Confirm the prior day's problem set was graded by the pod sub-leader against its rubric (IM-2)
  before the 9:00 kickoff, so the day's chapter lands on a cohort already aware of yesterday's
  highest-signal pitfall (IM-3).
- Run the 4:00 pm cohort sync ruthlessly — no day ends without standups, even on Friday with the
  show-and-tell.
- Push every chapter recording, every Monday mentor recording, and every cohort-sync recording to
  the wiki the same evening. The recording is the equity guarantee for any student who lost
  bandwidth, had a family emergency, or had an accommodation that needed asynchronous review (IM-6).

Each Monday:

- Open with the all-hands mentor session (9:00–10:00); record; post-session reflection prompt
  assigned by 10:00 for Friday review.
- Sub-leaders deliver one-sentence pod status to Prof. Gao by 9:00 — *who shipped Friday's
  deliverable, who needs intervention this week*.

Each Friday:

- Grade the Friday assessment against IM-2 by Saturday so the following Monday's all-hands can debrief
  from the cohort's own work, not generic examples.
- 4:30–4:50 show-and-tell: one randomly drawn student demos the week's deliverable to the cohort.
  This is graded only as participation, but the cohort knows their name might be drawn — the
  *possibility* is what holds attention through Friday afternoon.

Week 8 specifically:

- Tue Aug 11: dry-run defense for every student (sub-leaders + Prof. Gao). IM-4 gives the question
  protocol.
- Wed Aug 12: Symposium. Virtual breakouts open by 9:00 ET; in-person poster session per the College
  of Science schedule.
- Final week of the four-week paper-refinement window (for students writing the full paper): run
  `make clean && make all` on every capstone repo. It is the single most objective row in the
  terminal rubric, and it is the JSSR submission standard.

The clock is unforgiving precisely because the deliverable is real: these posters and abstracts head
to a real Symposium audience and a real journal. Pace the camp so that the freeze holds, the code
rebuilds, and every student can say the sentence the whole program exists to let them say truthfully
— *here is what I found, here is why you should believe it, and here is the command that lets you
check me.*
