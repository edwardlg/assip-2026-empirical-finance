Week 8 Assessment — The Capstone (Terminal Assessment of ASSIP)

This is the last thing you will be graded on, and it is the only thing the eight weeks of ASSIP were ever building toward. Weeks 1 through 4 handed you a toolkit; Weeks 5 and 6 taught you to read an estimator in a stranger’s hands and to build a measurement honestly; Week 7 made you design a falsifiable question of your own and file the pre-analysis plan and identification memo that froze it before the data could flatter you. Week 8 is where that frozen plan becomes the ASSIP terminal deliverable: a research poster a roomful of GMU faculty, mentors, families, and visiting College of Science leadership cannot ambush at the Aug 12 symposium; a JSSR abstract that survives the peer review of GMU Mason Publishing’s Journal of Student-Scientists’ Research; and a replication packet a stranger can rebuild with one command. The Week-7 assessment graded the design half and told you, in plain words, that its rubric is the rubric your capstone meets. This assessment is that promise come due. It extends the Week-7 research-design rubric to the full project: not just “is the plan one a referee could not ambush?” but “did you execute it honestly, stress-test it until it nearly died, distill it onto a poster so a stranger walking the floor believes it in ninety seconds, write a 200–300-word abstract a JSSR editor would accept, and ship a packet a stranger can check?”

Total: 200 points across four deliverables and seven rubric rows. The standard is the one this whole book has pressed since Chapter 1.5: a modest result you have stress-tested honestly outscores an ambitious one you cannot defend. A capstone that writes, unprompted, “my Oster \hat\delta is 0.4, so an unobservable weaker than my controls could explain the result, and I report it as a robust association, not a causal effect” earns more than one that claims a clean causal effect from the same data. Hold yourself to CONVENTIONS §4 (every spec named in seven-slot form) and §5 (the repo runs on a fresh env, the environment is pinned, licensed data stays on GMU infrastructure, no secret ever appears in code) throughout — those are not extra credit, they are the floor.

The ASSIP deliverable model, in one paragraph. ASSIP 2026 (Aspiring Scientists Summer Internship Program; George Mason University, College of Science; director Dr. Amanda Haymond Still; contact cosassip@gmu.edu) asks for a research poster at the in-person/virtual Aug 12 symposium, an abstract published in JSSR (Journal of Student-Scientists’ Research, GMU Mason Publishing), an optional full paper for students who want to push the work further, and participation in the program’s scientific-writing and career-forum sessions. The rubric below weights the four graded artifacts — the JSSR abstract, the poster, the 8-minute poster talk, and the replication packet — so that a clean, honest poster on a stress-tested result outscores an ambitious paper-style overclaim. The graded deliverable is the poster + abstract + talk + packet, not a full manuscript; the full-paper option is mentioned in Lab 8 for students who choose to write one.

Before you start, read one finished example in the capstone gallery (book/capstones/): five complete exemplar papers, each paired with the talk it underpins and the one-click packet that proves it — Fair Lending on HMDA, Common Ownership from 13F, Innovation from USPTO, SEC 8-K Text Classification, and a FRED macro event study. Each exemplar paper carries a top-of-document note that explains the framing: the ASSIP terminal deliverable is a poster + JSSR abstract, not a full paper, and the exemplar is the full research narrative the poster’s six panels and the abstract’s 250 words distill from. Read one not for its result but for its seams: find its identification section, find the residual concern its threats table admits, and run its make all. That is the bar you are distilling onto your poster.

How you submit

The four deliverables below are one thing seen from four sides: the abstract is the single-paragraph argument JSSR will publish; the poster is the visual argument the symposium audience walks up to; the talk is the eight-minute defense you give standing beside it; and the packet + disclosure is the proof a stranger can rebuild every number. You submit them together as the single Git repository you have been committing into since Lab 7 — the pap-filed tag still in its history, the confirmatory commit dated after it, DEVIATIONS.md filled in, the compiled paper/poster.pdf and the slide deck both regenerable by the same run_all that produces your results, and the JSSR-ready paper/abstract.tex generated from a single Python call so its 250 words cannot drift from the analysis they summarize. There is no separate “final upload”: the repository is the submission, and the first thing a grader does is clone it and type one command. Everything else — the rubric below, the question period at the Aug 12 symposium — is downstream of whether that command works. Treat the week as the assembly job it is, not as four new things to build: every piece already exists from Weeks 7 and 8; the capstone is the act of making them hold together under a stranger’s hands and on a 48”×36” board.

The four deliverables

D1 — The JSSR abstract (200–300 words, GMU Mason Publishing format)

The single most-read paragraph of your entire ASSIP, and the one that lives permanently in the Journal of Student-Scientists’ Research index whether or not you submit a full paper. Written in the fixed shape Ch 8.3 §2 trains for an introduction, compressed: (a) one sentence of motivation naming a concrete stake (the hook), (b) one sentence stating the question, (c) one sentence naming the data and design — including the identifying assumption verb (causal vs. associated) — (d) the headline number with its uncertainty in human units (e.g., “1.8 percentage points, about a fifth of the pre-period gap, 95% CI [-2.5, -1.1]”), (e) one sentence on the strongest robustness result, and (f) one sentence on what you are not claiming. 200–300 words inclusive. Generated by code, never typed — nb8.5 writes paper/abstract.tex from the frozen analysis variables (the same source as the poster’s headline panel), so the abstract and the poster cannot silently disagree. The verb is calibrated to your weakest assumption per Ch 8.3 §7; overclaiming here is the most expensive sentence you write in ASSIP, because JSSR’s audience sees this paragraph forever and almost no one will read past it.

D2 — The research poster (PDF, ~48”×36” landscape, GMU College of Science template)

The visual argument a stranger walks up to at the Aug 12 symposium and decides — in the eight seconds before they decide whether to stay — whether to ask you a question. Built on the GMU College of Science academic-poster template if one is distributed (search the ASSIP and College of Science sites; if no template is available the camp distributes a defensible landscape default; [CHECK] — confirm the current template URL with cosassip@gmu.edu), and arranged in the six-panel reading order that mirrors Ch 8.5’s six-beat talk arc:

  1. Title block — your name, ASSIP 2026, Prof. Lei Gao (mentor), the JSSR-ready 200–300-word abstract as a left-column block (the same \input{abstract.tex} the JSSR submission carries).
  2. Question + why it matters — one sentence each, large, top-left; concrete stake before any abstraction (CONVENTIONS §1).
  3. Data + design / identification — the seven-slot specification (CONVENTIONS §4) in compact form, and the identifying-assumption sentence on screen as the contract form: “our estimate is credible as long as [the assumption], which we defend by [the design].” This is the spine panel; if a passerby reads only this panel, they should still understand why the comparison is fair.
  4. The line between an A and a B capstone. Both run a real design on real data, find a result, and write it up cleanly; the bones are sound for both. The difference is honest stress-testing under pressure. The A capstone marks its pre-registered point on the spec curve and it sits near the center; it reports the check that failed and narrows the claim accordingly; its Oster \delta comes with a defended R_{\max} and a sweep to 1; make clean && make all rebuilds byte-for-byte; and in the defense the student concedes the one fatal critique rather than bluffing and can state precisely where their own design is weakest. The B capstone runs the battery but only shows the passes, asserts R_{\max} without the sweep, leaves the spec-curve point unmarked, has a packet that rebuilds “after one fix,” and in the defense argues harder when it should concede. The B is not dishonest — it simply has not yet internalized that a failed check is a finding and a conceded critique is a strength. That internalization is the whole point of the camp, and it is the A.

Common failure modes, the classic capstone ways to lose points: (a) Transcript, not argument — a chronological “first I downloaded, then I cleaned, then I tried three specs” with no headline-first structure; cap writing & table craft (Ch 8.3 §1). (b) Causal-language drift — abstract hedges, conclusion claims “proves”; read only the verbs attached to the main result across sections and force them to the weakest assumption (Ch 8.3 §7). (c) The robustness section that only passes — a battery with no failed check reads as less credible, because the grader assumes the student did not try hard enough to fail (Ch 8.2 §6); the scary placebo for the top-ranked threat must be present whether or not it passed. (d) The few-clusters illusion — three conventional stars on a treatment with eight treated clusters and no wild cluster bootstrap; if the bootstrap was run and disagreed, the stars must be dropped, and keeping them caps execution & robustness (Ch 8.2 §1). (e) Oster \delta without R_{\max} — uninterpretable; a \delta reported without its R_{\max} and a sweep to 1 is a non-answer (Ch 8.2 §5). (f) The pasted number — a table or figure copied by hand so the chain breaks and make clean && make all does not reproduce it; this is the most common reproducibility failure and it caps the packet row (Ch 8.5 §5). (g) The freeze broken — a confirmatory coefficient that postdates no tag or mutated after peeking; the p-value no longer means what Chapter 1.5 says, and the result is a search dressed as a test. (h) A hard-coded secret — caps reproducibility at Missing on its own, no exceptions, per §5.

ASSIP's poster session is in-person for on-campus interns and on virtual breakouts for remote
interns (Prof. Gao's 2026 ASSIP slot is officially remote, so the symposium's virtual track is the
primary defense venue), and the poster *is* your visual aid: each beat maps directly to a poster
panel, and you point at the panel as you speak. Optional companion slides (≤ 7) may be used for the
virtual track or for backup; if used, every result you say out loud is translated from a table into
a *figure* (point-with-error-bar, coefficient plot, event-study plot, RD binned scatter) — the Ch
8.5 §2 table-to-figure translation. Rehearse timed to fit in **seven** minutes with a pre-decided
cut line. The defense is graded as hard as the talk: the questions are the rows of your Chapter 7.5
threats table, so you answer in threat → why-plausible → what-you-did → residual-concern order, you
give a *located* "I don't know" when you cannot answer, and you can tell a survivable critique (it
attacks a number) from a fatal one (it attacks the comparison) and concede the fatal one honestly
rather than bluff.

### D5 — The responsible-AI-use disclosure (from Week 6)

A short disclosure (Ch 6.5 / Assessment 6 B4) stating, by category, where AI tools touched the work:
**label generation or any AI-produced data needs a validation table; prose editing is disclosed but
needs no validation; AI-suggested citations are the most dangerous and must be independently verified
or tagged `[CHECK]`.** This is the camp's honesty norm made explicit, and per CONVENTIONS §5 no key
or secret appears anywhere — environment variables only.

whole project and is weighted, by design, toward **identification and threats**, **execution and
robustness**, and **reproducibility** — because those, not the eventual coefficient, are what make a
capstone defensible. The first two rows carry forward the Week-7 weights (they are the design half you
already filed); the new weight goes to *honest execution* and to the two ASSIP-specific

## Instructor guidance and grading key

This is an open-ended terminal deliverable on each student's own project, so the key is a set of
*standards*, anchor papers, and the bright lines that separate the levels — not an answer sheet.

**The two highest-signal things to grade**, the same two from Week 7 now joined by execution: (1)
**Was there exactly one pre-registered primary specification, run once on the confirmation data and
reported whichever way it fell**, with the `pap-filed` tag predating any confirmatory commit? Check
the Git history; a starred headline that postdates no tag, or a primary spec that mutated after the
first look, breaks the contract and caps *execution & robustness* at Developing. (2) **Does
`make clean && make all` actually rebuild the paper's numbers on a fresh clone?** Run it. This is not
a formality — it is the row, and it is the single most objective thing in the rubric. A packet that
does not rebuild caps reproducibility at Developing; a hard-coded secret caps it at Missing on its own
per CONVENTIONS §5.

**How to grade fairly.** Grade the project the student *chose to write*, not the one you would have
written (the refereeing asymmetry of Ch 8.4 §1). Ambition is not the axis — a clean OLS-on-observables
study that honestly reports its Oster $\delta$ and labels its claim "robust association" is an A
project; an ambitious DiD that overclaims "causes" with a blank residual column is not. Calibrate
against the gallery: each of the five capstones is a worked anchor at the A/A− line. Grade each row
independently against the level descriptors, then sum; do not let a brilliant talk inflate a packet
that does not rebuild, and do not let one missing placebo sink an otherwise honest battery — that is
what the four-level structure is for.

**The line between an A and a B capstone.** Both run a real design on real data, find a result, and
write it up cleanly; the bones are sound for both. The difference is *honest stress-testing under
pressure*. The A capstone marks its pre-registered point on the spec curve and it sits near the
center; it reports the check that *failed* and narrows the claim accordingly; its Oster $\delta$ comes
with a defended $R_{\max}$ and a sweep to 1; `make clean && make all` rebuilds byte-for-byte; and in
the defense the student concedes the one fatal critique rather than bluffing and can state precisely
where their own design is weakest. The B capstone runs the battery but only shows the passes, asserts
$R_{\max}$ without the sweep, leaves the spec-curve point unmarked, has a packet that rebuilds "after
one fix," and in the defense argues harder when it should concede. The B is not dishonest — it simply
has not yet internalized that a failed check is a finding and a conceded critique is a strength. That
internalization is the whole point of the camp, and it is the A.

**Common failure modes**, the classic capstone ways to lose points:
(a) *Transcript, not argument* — a chronological "first I downloaded, then I cleaned, then I tried
three specs" with no headline-first structure; cap *writing & table craft* (Ch 8.3 §1).
(b) *Causal-language drift* — abstract hedges, conclusion claims "proves"; read only the verbs
attached to the main result across sections and force them to the weakest assumption (Ch 8.3 §7).
(c) *The robustness section that only passes* — a battery with no failed check reads as *less*
credible, because the grader assumes the student did not try hard enough to fail (Ch 8.2 §6); the
scary placebo for the top-ranked threat must be present whether or not it passed.
(d) *The few-clusters illusion* — three conventional stars on a treatment with eight treated clusters
and no wild cluster bootstrap; if the bootstrap was run and disagreed, the stars must be dropped, and
keeping them caps *execution & robustness* (Ch 8.2 §1).
(e) *Oster $\delta$ without $R_{\max}$* — uninterpretable; a $\delta$ reported without its $R_{\max}$
and a sweep to 1 is a non-answer (Ch 8.2 §5).
(f) *The pasted number* — a table or figure copied by hand so the chain breaks and `make clean &&
make all` does not reproduce it; this is the most common reproducibility failure and it caps the
packet row (Ch 8.5 §5).
(g) *The freeze broken* — a confirmatory coefficient that postdates no tag or mutated after peeking;
the p-value no longer means what Chapter 1.5 says, and the result is a search dressed as a test.
(h) *A hard-coded secret* — caps reproducibility at Missing on its own, no exceptions, per §5.

**How the rubric maps to the publication standard.** This capstone is calibrated to the
real-program bar: ASSIP's required deliverable is an **abstract published in the Journal of
Student-Scientists' Research (JSSR), GMU Mason Publishing** plus a **poster presented at the
Aug 12, 2026 symposium**, with an optional full paper. An *Excellent* across the contribution
(abstract), identification, execution, poster, and reproducibility rows is, concretely, a poster +
abstract pair that would clear a first-round JSSR review and that a symposium judge would not be
able to ambush: a credible (not overclaimed) identifying assumption with a threats table that
disarms the obvious attacks, a robustness battery that a reviewer would otherwise demand in round
two, figures legible from six feet and built to the Appendix-D craft standard, and a one-click
packet that satisfies the JSSR data-availability expectation. The JSSR submission standard *is* the
reproducibility row: a stranger scans the poster's QR code, clones the repo, types one command, and
the abstract and headline number rebuild from the deposited data and code. Grade to that standard,
because that is where the strongest ASSIP outputs are actually headed.

### What a top ASSIP capstone looks like, in one paragraph

It opens — both on the poster's top-left panel and in the JSSR abstract's first sentence — with a
hook that leads with a fact and a stake, and a one-sentence contribution whose verb is calibrated
to a design the audience can see filling a real, *constructed* gap; the design panel of the poster
shows the identifying-assumption sentence (effect named, specific threat named, verb honest) in
contract form, backed by a threats table that leads with the most dangerous row, matches a
statistic to every testable threat and an argument to every arguable one, and leaves no residual
cell empty; the headline panel reports the pre-registered spec — run once and reported whichever
way it fell — as a figure with its confidence interval and a single human-units sentence, with the
specification curve from Ch 8.1 in the robustness panel showing the marked pre-registered point
near the center of the distribution and an Oster $\delta$ with a defended, swept $R_{\max}$ noted
honestly, including the one check that narrowed the claim; the abstract carries the same number
because the abstract is *generated* from the same analysis variables — they cannot disagree; it
ships as a repo where `make clean && make all` rebuilds the **poster PDF** and the **abstract**
from raw data through seeded code with no secret anywhere, and the **QR code in the poster's
bottom-right panel** resolves to that repo on a visiting reviewer's phone; it is defended in eight
minutes of figures-not-tables standing beside the poster (or on the symposium's virtual breakout
track, since Prof. Gao's 2026 slot is remote), with the design panel as the spine, and in the
question period the student answers from their own threats table, gives a located "I don't know,"
and concedes the one fatal critique rather than bluffing; and it discloses, by category, exactly
where AI touched the work. That capstone can say the sentence the whole eight weeks existed to let
a student say truthfully, standing beside the poster on Aug 12: *"Here is what I found, here is
exactly why you should believe it, and here is the QR code that lets you check me."* That is an
original ASSIP capstone. That is the A.
