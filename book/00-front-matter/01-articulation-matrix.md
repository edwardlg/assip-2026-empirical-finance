# Articulation Matrix: The 8-Week Camp and the ASSIP 2026 Calendar

## How the two fit together

The **Aspiring Scientists Summer Internship Program (ASSIP)** at George Mason University's College of Science is a full-time, eight-week REU-style apprenticeship that runs Monday through Friday, 9:00 am to 5:00 pm, from Monday, June 22, 2026 through the **ASSIP Research Symposium on Wednesday, August 12, 2026** (orientation Thursday, June 18; holidays observed Friday, June 19 for Juneteenth and Friday, July 3 for Independence Day). It is directed by Dr. Amanda Haymond Still (cosassip@gmu.edu), accepts high-school students aged 15 and older (16+ for in-person wet-lab placements) as well as undergraduates not yet graduated, sets no GPA minimum, allows a maximum of two summer sessions, costs \$25 to apply plus \$1,299 tuition with full waivers for Pell-eligible and reduced-lunch students, and awards three GMU College of Science credits on completion. Prof. Gao's 2026 placement is officially **remote-only**, which makes the operational discipline of this camp — version-controlled code, written pre-analysis plans, asynchronous code review — load-bearing rather than optional. The mentor model assigns one faculty mentor per group; with a cohort sitting at the high end of typical ASSIP group sizes (the program ranges from 2 to 15 students per group), the camp is staffed with layered mentorship — Prof. Gao as faculty mentor, PhD students and research assistants as sub-leaders — so that every intern gets contact time, feedback on writing, and a careful read of their identifying assumption.

The two pieces of the experience are deliberately complementary. **ASSIP frames:** it supplies the real research project, the institutional credibility of a Mason College of Science program, the layered mentorship, the symposium deliverable, the JSSR publication track, the scientific-writing forum, the career programming, and the transcript line. **The camp deepens:** it supplies the econometric rigor — probability and inference, OLS and its standard-error zoo, the causal-inference toolkit, the identification reading discipline, and the AI co-pilot module — that turns a summer placement into a defensible paper rather than a slide deck. A student doing ASSIP alone gets an apprenticeship; a student doing the camp inside ASSIP gets an apprenticeship plus the methods to answer their own research question honestly.

## What ASSIP brings that the camp content does not

The camp is a textbook with notebooks and problem sets. ASSIP is an institution. The five things ASSIP supplies that no book can are:

1. **Layered mentorship.** One faculty mentor per group, with PhD students and research assistants as sub-leaders, gives every intern multiple humans reading their work — a faculty perspective on the research question, a graduate-student perspective on the methods, an RA perspective on the day-to-day code. The camp's content is consumed alone or in pairs; ASSIP's mentorship is consumed in conversation.
2. **The JSSR publication track.** The *Journal of Student-Scientists' Research*, hosted by GMU Mason Publishing, is a real venue with a real archival record. Every ASSIP intern is expected to submit at minimum an abstract; the optional full paper goes through a student-scientist peer-review process. Nothing in a textbook gets archived in Mason Publishing.
3. **The 3-credit transcript line.** Three GMU College of Science credits show up on an official transcript — they articulate to other universities, they count toward dual-enrollment GPAs, and they signal that an outside institution has vouched for the work.
4. **The symposium.** Wednesday, August 12, 2026, an in-person poster session at GMU (with virtual breakouts for remote interns including Prof. Gao's 2026 group), where Mason faculty, mentors, families, College of Science leadership, and Mason News press read your work and ask hard questions. The textbook ends in a problem set; ASSIP ends in a defense.
5. **Institutional credibility.** "I worked with a faculty mentor at George Mason University's College of Science in the Aspiring Scientists Summer Internship Program" is a sentence a 16-year-old can put on a college application, and it lands differently than "I read a book." That credibility is what the \$25 application + \$1,299 tuition (or, for Pell-eligible / reduced-lunch students, the full waiver) actually buys.

## What the camp content brings that an ASSIP placement alone might not

ASSIP is full-time and intense, but the methodological content varies by mentor and lab. A finance-and-econometrics placement without a structured curriculum can end up teaching one project's methods rather than a field's methods. The five things the camp supplies that a generic placement might miss are:

1. **A structured 8-week curriculum** of econometric methods, sequenced from probability and OLS through causal inference and frontier reading, deliberately aligned to the ASSIP calendar above.
2. **Approximately 40 reproducible Jupyter notebooks** — one per major concept, every dataset publicly accessible, every result reproducible from a fresh clone of the repository.
3. **Approximately 40 problem sets** with answer keys, sequenced so that the Friday problem set previews the next Monday's lecture rather than recapping the last one.
4. **Five worked capstone exemplars** — five end-to-end research projects with data, code, paper, and poster, so that an intern doesn't enter ASSIP guessing at what "done" looks like.
5. **The AI co-pilot module** — a deliberate, ethics-first treatment of how to use Claude, ChatGPT, and Copilot as research assistants: where they help (boilerplate, syntax, regex, prose polishing), where they hurt (made-up citations, plausible-but-wrong identification arguments), and how to keep a paper trail of what the model did versus what you did.

## The handshake

The book is the textbook. The wiki (see `/wiki/`) is the operational portal — the place that holds the ASSIP-specific logistics, the rolling weekly agenda, the office-hours schedule, the Slack norms, the data-access tickets, the symposium templates, and the JSSR submission walkthrough. The GitHub repository holds both — the book sources and the wiki content — and it also holds each intern's pre-analysis plan as a tagged commit (`pap-v1.0` filed at the end of Week 5), so that "what you said you would do" is provably timestamped against "what you ended up doing." When the August 12 symposium poster goes up, the reviewer who wants to see whether the result was pre-specified or fished can pull up the Week-5 commit and verify it in thirty seconds. That handshake — textbook in the book, logistics in the wiki, evidence in the repo — is what lets a high-school cohort produce work that an econometrician will read seriously.
