    Reading list — the ten anchor papers¶

    Two halves, both required:

    • Five classics — the empirical-finance papers that defined the field's methods (Weeks 5 and 6).
    • Five Gao papers — Prof. Gao's own publications, which thread the program's theme of fair lending, innovation, and rigorous identification (referenced from Weeks 3 – 8).

    Stable links are JSTOR, SSRN, the publisher's DOI, or the journal page. Where a paper is paywalled and the author's working-paper version is publicly hosted, both links are given.

    The five classics¶

    •   Fama & French (1992) — The Cross-Section of Expected Stock Returns

      Journal of Finance 47(2): 427–465. JSTOR

      Why it matters: the paper that killed beta and put size + book-to-market on the asset-pricing map. Read first, before FF93. Week 5.1.

    •   Fama & French (1993) — Common Risk Factors in the Returns on Stocks and Bonds

      Journal of Financial Economics 33(1): 3–56. DOI

      Why it matters: the three-factor model itself, with the GRS test, time-series regressions, and the template that every multi-factor paper since 1993 has copied. Week 5.2.

    •   Jegadeesh & Titman (1993) — Returns to Buying Winners and Selling Losers

      Journal of Finance 48(1): 65–91. JSTOR

      Why it matters: momentum is the anomaly that wouldn't die. The overlapping-portfolio construction is a methods lesson on its own. Week 5.3.

    •   Petersen (2009) — Estimating Standard Errors in Finance Panel Data Sets

      Review of Financial Studies 22(1): 435–480. DOI

      Why it matters: the paper everyone cites but few read carefully. We read it as a paper, not as a methods note — the empirical exhibit driving the t-stat from 5.70 to 2.19 is the point. Week 5.4 + cited throughout Week 2.

    •   Bertrand, Duflo & Mullainathan (2004) — How Much Should We Trust Differences-in-Differences Estimates?

      Quarterly Journal of Economics 119(1): 249–275. DOI

      Why it matters: the placebo-law experiment that exposed serial correlation in DiD inference. Set the standard for clustered standard errors. Week 5.5.

    •   Kogan, Papanikolaou, Seru & Stoffman (2017) — Technological Innovation, Resource Allocation, and Growth

      Quarterly Journal of Economics 132(2): 665–712. DOI

      Why it matters: market-based patent value — the construct that turned patent counts into something economists could actually use as a variable. Week 6.1.

    •   Hoberg & Phillips (2016) — Text-Based Network Industries and Endogenous Product Differentiation

      Journal of Political Economy 124(5): 1423–1465. DOI

      Why it matters: the paper that legitimized text-as-data in finance and gave us the TNIC industry classification still used today. Week 6.2.

    •   Loughran & McDonald (2011) — When Is a Liability Not a Liability? Textual Analysis, Dictionaries, and 10-Ks

      Journal of Finance 66(1): 35–65. DOI

      Why it matters: the finance sentiment dictionary that replaced Harvard-IV in every serious 10-K text paper since. Read it, then use the dictionary in Lab 6. Week 6.3.

    Five Gao papers¶

    These five trace the methodological arc of the program — fair lending, banking, innovation — through Prof. Gao's own work.

    •   Gao, Hwang & Liu — Lender screening, illiquidity, and the disclosure premium

      See the Quarto textbook bibliography for the canonical citation and the publicly available working-paper SSRN link.

      Why it matters: the foundation paper for our fair-lending and bank-screening discussions. Week-3 PSM examples ride on its empirical setup.

    •   Gao & Sun (2019) — Fair lending and algorithmic mortgage pricing

      Cited in the Week 6.4 Reader's Guide. SSRN link in the textbook bibliography.

      Why it matters: the over-controlling trap in HMDA-based fair-lending studies, made concrete. Lab 3 and Lab 6 both build on it.

    •   Gao et al. (2024) — Innovation, patents, and the cross-section of returns (published 2024)

      See the textbook bibliography for the published JF / JFE / RFS citation.

      Why it matters: extends KPSS (2017) and shows where the patent-value construct still has identifying-assumption gaps. Week 6 reading.

    •   Gao — Bank regulation and small-business lending

      Citation and link in the textbook bibliography.

      Why it matters: a worked staggered-DiD design that Week 4 leans on as a case study.

    •   Gao — Most recent working paper

      SSRN link in the textbook bibliography; updated each summer.

      Why it matters: read in Week 7 as a model of how a research question turns into a registered design — the version we want your capstone to imitate.

    Gao paper citations

    Gao paper titles, journals, page ranges, and DOIs are stored once in the Quarto textbook's bibliography file so we don't have two places to update them. The Quarto build is the single source of truth — if a citation here ever disagrees with the textbook, the textbook is right.

    How to access these papers¶

    • JSTOR — free through your GMU library proxy. Sign in with your @gmu.edu account.
    • SSRN — public; no auth needed.
    • Publisher DOIs (Elsevier, OUP, Univ. Chicago Press, Wiley) — most are open through the GMU library; if you hit a paywall, sign in via the Fenwick Library off-campus access.
    • Ken French Data Library — public; no auth. Used in Lab 5 (FF93 replication).

    Read the Reader's Guide chapter before the paper. The chapter is the trail map; the paper is the trail.
